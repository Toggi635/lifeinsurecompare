1. Introduction

One of the most common questions people ask is: "How much life insurance do I need?" The answer depends on your unique financial situation, family obligations, and long-term goals. Buy too little and your family may struggle financially. Buy too much and you waste money on premiums you do not need.

The good news is that calculating your ideal coverage amount is not complicated. By breaking down your financial obligations into clear categories, you can arrive at a number that gives you confidence your family will be protected.

Key Takeaway: Most families need between 10 and 15 times their annual income in life insurance coverage. However, the most accurate approach is to calculate your specific needs using the DIME method outlined below.

2. The DIME Method

The DIME method is a simple framework for calculating your life insurance needs. It stands for:

  • Debt — All outstanding debts beyond your mortgage
  • Income replacement — How much your family needs to maintain their lifestyle
  • Mortgage — Any remaining mortgage balance
  • Education — Future education costs for your children

Add these four categories together, then add final expenses, and you have your target coverage amount.

3. Income Replacement

Income replacement is typically the largest component of your life insurance needs. The goal is to provide enough money that your family can maintain their current lifestyle without your income.

How Much Income Replacement?

A common guideline is to replace 70% to 80% of your after-tax income for the number of years your family would need support. Most financial advisors recommend 10 to 12 times your annual income as a starting point.

Income Replacement Example

Annual Income Replacement Factor Total Income Replacement
$50,000 10x $500,000
$75,000 10x $750,000
$100,000 12x $1,200,000
$150,000 12x $1,800,000
$250,000+ 15x $3,750,000

How to Invest the Death Benefit: A $1,000,000 death benefit invested at 5% annual return would generate $50,000 per year in interest — allowing the principal to last indefinitely. This is why 20x to 25x annual expenses is a common target for full income replacement.

4. Debt and Mortgage Coverage

Non-Mortgage Debt

Add up all outstanding debts: car loans, student loans, credit card balances, personal loans, and any other obligations. Your life insurance should be sufficient to pay off all of these so your family is not burdened with debt payments.

Mortgage

Most families want life insurance to pay off the mortgage if the primary breadwinner dies. Include your remaining mortgage balance in your coverage calculation. Some people prefer a smaller policy that covers mortgage payments for a few years rather than paying off the entire balance.

5. Education Expenses

If you have children, factor in the cost of their future education. For 2026, the average cost of a four-year public university (tuition, room, board) is approximately $110,000 per child. Private universities can cost $200,000 or more per child.

Multiply the estimated cost by the number of children you have and add this to your coverage target. Many parents include full funding for college as part of their life insurance plan.

6. Final Expenses

Final expense coverage covers the costs associated with your death: funeral expenses, medical bills not covered by insurance, estate settlement costs, and legal fees. A typical funeral costs $8,000 to $15,000. Add $10,000 to $20,000 to your coverage amount for final expenses.

7. Do Stay-at-Home Parents Need Life Insurance?

Yes. Stay-at-home parents provide valuable services that would cost $50,000 to $100,000 or more per year to replace: childcare, transportation, housekeeping, meal preparation, and more. If a stay-at-home parent dies, the surviving parent would need to pay for these services or reduce their work hours.

A reasonable coverage amount for a stay-at-home parent is $250,000 to $500,000, depending on the age and number of children. This covers childcare costs and household services until children are in school full-time.

8. Sample Calculation: The Johnson Family

Let us calculate life insurance needs for a real example: Mark (age 38, earns $85,000/year), Sarah (stay-at-home parent), two children (ages 4 and 6), remaining mortgage balance of $280,000.

Category DETAILS Amount
Debt Car loan, student loans, credit cards $45,000
Income Replacement $85,000 x 10 years $850,000
Mortgage Remaining balance $280,000
Education $110,000 x 2 children $220,000
Final Expenses Funeral, estate costs $15,000
TOTAL $1,410,000

The Johnsons need approximately $1.4 million in life insurance for Mark. In practice, they might buy a $1 million 30-year term policy and a $500,000 20-year term policy to ladder their coverage and save on premiums compared to buying a single $1.5 million 30-year policy.

9. When to Adjust Your Coverage

Your life insurance needs change over time. Review your coverage whenever these life events occur:

  • Marriage: Add coverage for your new spouse and combined obligations
  • Having a child: Increase coverage to include education and childcare costs
  • Buying a home: Add mortgage coverage
  • Salary increase: Increase income replacement accordingly
  • Children become independent: May reduce coverage needs
  • Mortgage paid off: Can reduce coverage
  • Divorce: Update beneficiaries and coverage amounts

Review your life insurance coverage annually during open enrollment or when you review your overall financial plan. Term policies can be supplemented with additional policies as your needs grow.

10. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the DIME method for life insurance?

DIME stands for Debt, Income replacement, Mortgage, and Education. This method calculates your life insurance needs by adding up these four categories of financial obligations, then adding final expenses.

How many times my salary should my life insurance be?

A common rule of thumb is 10 to 12 times your annual income. However, your actual needs depend on your debts, mortgage, children's education costs, and how long your family needs income replacement.

Do stay-at-home parents need life insurance?

Yes. Stay-at-home parents provide valuable services worth $50,000 to $100,000+ per year. Life insurance for them covers childcare, housekeeping, and other services the surviving spouse would need to pay for.

Should I buy one policy or multiple policies?

Many people use a laddering strategy: buy multiple term policies with different term lengths. For example, a 30-year policy for long-term needs and a 20-year policy for shorter-term obligations. This can save money compared to buying one large 30-year policy.

How often should I review my coverage?

Review your life insurance coverage annually or whenever you experience a major life event: marriage, having a child, buying a home, getting a raise, or divorce.
