1. Why Annual Review Matters

Your life insurance policy is not a set-it-and-forget-it financial product. Life changes — marriage, children, career moves, health changes, and retirement — all affect how much coverage you need and whether your current policy remains the best option. An annual policy review ensures your coverage keeps pace with your life.

According to industry surveys, nearly 40% of policyholders have never reviewed their life insurance coverage after purchasing it. This means millions of families are either overpaying for coverage they no longer need or leaving their loved ones underinsured at a critical time. Annual reviews close this gap.

Beyond coverage amounts, policy reviews help you take advantage of changing market conditions. Life insurance rates have become increasingly competitive, and your current premium may no longer be the best deal available. A simple comparison could save you hundreds of dollars per year.

Key Takeaway: An annual 30-minute policy review can save you money, improve your coverage, and ensure your beneficiaries are correctly designated. Mark your calendar for the same date each year.

2. Coverage Adequacy Check

The most important part of your policy review is determining whether your current death benefit is sufficient. The coverage amount that made sense five years ago may be inadequate today due to inflation, lifestyle changes, and new financial obligations.

Calculate Your Current Coverage Needs

Use the DIME method (Debt, Income, Mortgage, Education) to estimate your current coverage needs. Add up your outstanding debts, multiply your annual income by the number of years you want to replace it, include your remaining mortgage balance, and factor in education costs for each child. Compare this total against your current death benefit.

If your current death benefit is significantly higher than your need, you may be overpaying and could consider reducing coverage. If there is a gap, consider purchasing additional term coverage to fill it.

3. Beneficiary Review

Beneficiary designations are legally binding and override instructions in your will. This makes it essential to review and update your beneficiaries whenever your family situation changes. An outdated beneficiary designation can cause your death benefit to go to the wrong person or create legal complications for your estate.

Beneficiary Checklist

  • Primary beneficiary: Verify the name, relationship, and contact information are current
  • Contingent beneficiaries: Ensure you have at least one contingent (secondary) beneficiary named
  • Minor children: If beneficiaries are minors, establish a trust or name a custodian under UGMA/UTMA
  • Divorce update: Update designations after divorce — many states revoke ex-spouse designations but not all
  • Per stirpes vs. per capita: Understand how your policy pays out if a beneficiary predeceases you

Special Needs Considerations

If you have a dependent with special needs, naming them directly as a beneficiary could disqualify them from government benefits like Medicaid or SSI. Instead, consider establishing a Special Needs Trust as your beneficiary to preserve their eligibility while still providing financial support.

4. Premium Comparison

Life insurance rates have been trending downward over the past decade due to improved mortality data, increased competition, and advances in medical underwriting. If you purchased your policy more than a few years ago, you may be able to find a lower premium for equivalent or better coverage.

When to Shop for New Coverage

  • Health improvements: If you have quit smoking, lost weight, or improved a chronic condition, you may qualify for preferred rates
  • Age considerations: Premiums increase with age, so if you are still young and healthy, locking in new coverage now may be beneficial
  • Policy type change: You may find that your needs have shifted from term to permanent, or vice versa
  • Rider needs: Newer policies may offer riders (critical illness, disability waiver) that your current policy lacks

5. Policy Features Audit

Beyond premium and coverage amount, your policy has features, riders, and provisions that should be reviewed annually. Many policyholders are unaware of options available within their existing policies, such as conversion rights, accelerated death benefits, or waiver of premium riders.

Riders to Evaluate

  • Accelerated Death Benefit (ADB): Allows you to access a portion of the death benefit if diagnosed with a terminal illness
  • Waiver of Premium (WP): Waives premiums if you become disabled and unable to work
  • Conversion Option: Allows converting term to permanent coverage without a medical exam
  • Child Rider: Provides small coverage amounts for each child, typically convertible to permanent policies later
  • Guaranteed Insurability: Allows you to purchase additional coverage at specific life events without underwriting

Policy Provisions Check

Review your policy's renewal provisions, especially if you have term insurance. Does your policy have guaranteed renewability? If so, you can renew at the end of your term without proving insurability — though premiums will be higher. Understanding these provisions helps you make informed decisions about whether to renew, convert, or replace your policy.

6. Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I review my life insurance?

You should review your life insurance policy at least once per year. Additionally, review after major life events such as marriage, divorce, birth of a child, mortgage, job change, or retirement.

What changes warrant a policy review?

Major life events that warrant a policy review include marriage, divorce, birth or adoption of a child, purchasing a home, changing jobs, receiving a significant raise, starting a business, or the death of a dependent.

Can I switch life insurance companies?

Yes, you can switch life insurance companies by applying for a new policy and canceling the old one. However, consider your health status first — if your health has declined, keeping your existing policy may be more cost-effective.

What happens if I stop paying premiums?

If you stop paying premiums on a term life policy, coverage ends after the grace period (typically 30 days). For permanent policies, the insurer may use cash value to continue coverage or convert to reduced paid-up insurance.

Should I update beneficiaries after divorce?

Yes, updating beneficiaries after divorce is critical. Many states automatically revoke a former spouse's beneficiary designation, but laws vary. Explicitly name new beneficiaries in writing to ensure your policy aligns with your current wishes.
